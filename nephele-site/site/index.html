Dirty ship emissions have brightened clouds for over a century.

Sulfur aerosol in ship exhaust seeded brighter marine clouds, reflecting heat. In 2020, clean-air regulations removed that aerosol — inadvertently resulting in a measurable increase in the rate of global ocean warming. The clouds dimmed. The planet got warmer.

Nephele puts the reflectivity back in ship emissions — deliberately, cleanly, with sea salt.

The Approach

The "dumb" solution — because it can be deployed now, with existing technology.

Every marine cloud brightening proposal has struggled with spiraling complexity. We're proposing the brute-force method: simply inject seawater spray into cargo ship exhaust, hijacking existing emissions scrubber architecture. The ship's exhaust plume does all the hard work — turbulent mixing, droplet evaporation, and a free ride to the cloud base.

No new science. No purpose-built fleet. No precision nanoparticle engineering. Just off-the-shelf hardware piggybacking on scrubber hardware that already exists on over 4,000 industrial vessels already at sea.

01

Inject brine at the stack exit

Simple pressure-atomizing nozzles spray filtered seawater into the cleaned exhaust, downstream of the scrubber. Tap into existing scrubber seawater plumbing, using less than 1% of its water throughput.

02

The plume does the hard work

Turbulent mixing disperses the sea salt spray. Evaporation produces salt crystals. Thermal buoyancy preferentially lofts the smallest, most effective cloud condensation nuclei naturally, while larger salt crystals fall out of suspension and back into the sea.

03

Clouds brighten

Salt crystals activate as cloud condensation nuclei, increasing droplet number and reflectivity via the Twomey effect. Onboard monitoring and satellite verification attributes reflected heat to individual vessels.

04

Measured, paid cooling

CERES/MODIS radiative forcing data verifies the cooling. We compensate participating vessels.

05

Immediate, total reversibility

Stop injection, and the effect ceases within hours. No persistent atmospheric modification, no long-term commitment. We are seeking to operate under full IMO MEPC oversight — cloud brightening should be tightly regulated, transparently verified, and institutionally governed.

Why This Works

Not geoengineering new clouds. Replacing lost ship tracks from IMO 2020.

Not new

We are restoring an effect that shipping itself produced for over a century, backed by research that has been well-understood for decades.

Not permanent

Salt aerosol atmospheric lifetime is hours to days. Cessation of injection immediately halts the effect. The strongest possible off switch.

Not sulfur

NaCl replaces SO₂ — we use a substance that belongs in the ocean with twice the hygroscopicity. The clean air benefits of IMO 2020 are fully preserved.

~$250K per vessel

Simple hardware, proven materials, installed during a routine port call.

4,000+ ships ready to deploy

Scrubber-equipped industrial vessels already have the plumbing and exhaust infrastructure to make this work.

Satellite-verified

NASA's CERES instruments provide independent, auditable radiative forcing measurement. Every watt is accounted for.
